The role of management and executive leadership

To positively influence workplace culture, leaders can model mentally healthy behaviours, proactively address stigma, and make action-based commitments to foster mental well-being in the organization and within teams.

It is important for management to view mental health as a collective priority rather than simply as an individual concern. Approaching mental health solely through individualized measures such as sick leave and health care benefits is insufficient.

Some ways in which leadership can support positive mental well-being at work include:

Examples of practices and resources for changing culture include:

  • using accountability mechanisms such as pulse surveys
  • encouraging leaders to serve as allies by sharing their personal experiences
  • offering training in navigating mental health at work, engaging in difficult conversations, and creating supportive workplaces
  • investing in equity, diversity and inclusion initiatives to support employee mental health and its intersectionality, especially 2SLGBTQI+ communities, people with disabilities, Indigenous Peoples, Black employees, and other racialized people
  • creating employee-led peer initiatives to share resources and promote listening
  • encouraging employees to become mental health champions

Working in sustainable ways

Promoting work flexibility, respecting boundaries and autonomy, and creating norms around communication can go a long way towards building a mentally healthy culture.

See examples

Examples include:

  • encouraging direct reports and managers to discuss their working styles and preferences
  • providing the resources (such as bandwidth) that teams need to conduct their work effectively without stress
  • promoting no-meeting days, focused work time, and no emails after hours to allow employees to work in the most productive way

Connecting more meaningfully

A culture of deeper connections and meaningful interactions within teams and between employees and employers can help employees feel supported, included, and able to share what is important to them. The COVID-19 pandemic has, in some ways, paved the way for greater compassion and empathy in addressing accommodation needs, finding sustainable ways of working, and respecting the different ways people navigate the work-life balance.
