Microaggressions and mental health

In addition to overt racism and discrimination, marginalized people can repeatedly experience covert or subtle forms of discrimination known as microaggressions. This experience can negatively impact their mental health within and outside of the workplace. The following video describes what microaggressions are, what they can look like in the workplace, and how they can negatively impact our mental health and well-being.

Content warning

This video describes microaggressions, provides examples, discusses actions, and prompts reflection. As this content can potentially trigger negative memories and experiences, please prioritize your well-being and refer to the linked resources as needed.

Transcript

Microaggressions are characterized by the everyday subtle, intentional, and sometimes unintentional interactions or behaviours that show bias towards marginalized groups. People who commit microaggressions may not be aware of what they are. Here are some examples.

Microaggressions can include making assumptions about someone’s competence based on their first language or immigration status. Unintentionally excluding someone with a disability from participating in a team project is also a form of microaggression.

Dr. Jasmin Zine, a professor at Wilfrid Laurier University whose research focuses on anti-racism and Islamophobia education, explains that when it comes to microaggressions, “the intent is not the issue, it’s the impact.”[12]

Although seemingly subtle or benign, microaggressions can be just as damaging as overt forms of discrimination and can cause great damage to a person’s mental health over time.[13] They wear away at a person's self-confidence, self-esteem, and sense of belonging. Research shows that individuals who experience microaggressions often develop mental health symptoms like depression, anxiety, and a lack of behavioural control.[14]

Since the effects of microaggressions are cumulative, any seemingly harmless comment can become the final straw that breaks the camel’s back.[15] So, what does this mean?

Organizations need to have conversations about microaggressions and their harmful effects. We are all prone to making mistakes, and we might commit microaggressions. This does not mean we are bad people. Rather, it means we need to be more aware of our biases and their impacts on others. We need to actively work on our interactions, increase our knowledge, and engage in difficult conversations.[16]
