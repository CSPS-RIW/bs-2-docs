Considerations for managers

People’s individual, family and community histories can include events, unknown to others, that leave a mark and affect them every day. Almost two thirds of Canadians have been exposed to an event categorized as potentially traumatizing.[22]

Though identities and personal experiences vary within groups, marginalized groups often experience greater frequency and extents of mental health problems due to discrimination and harassment, which is prevalent in Western societies at individual, interpersonal, community, organizational and structural levels. Those with overlapping and intersecting identities are further disadvantaged because they can experience multiplicative, unique, and multi-level discrimination and harassment.

What this means in the workplace

Traumatic experiences usually happen in situations where there is a power differential. As a manager, it’s important to be aware of the power you have and its potential direct and indirect impacts on your employees.

Where possible, managers should aim for collaboration, information-sharing, and seeking and obtaining informed and meaningful consent. Decisions reached through collaboration are more likely to honour the interests of everyone involved, ensure employees' needs are addressed, and support employees in doing their best work.

Despite the difference in power, leveraging opportunities for shared decision-making with employees will help managers to build trust and contribute to improving psychological health and safety in the workplace.

Challenges and opportunities

Of course, shared decision-making between managers and employees is not always realistic in the workplace. Sometimes managers have to make decisions that impact their employees, or must implement and execute decisions made elsewhere in the organization.

How can respect be honoured when shared decision-making is not possible, and employees cannot counter or change a decision?

Here are a few approaches and considerations:

  • Be transparent about whether employees have a choice, why a decision has been made, and what the implications are.
  • Seriously consider employee input and feedback, especially when employees speak about themselves and when what they say counters your beliefs, expectations and worldviews.
  • Receive employee input with good grace and without judgment, treating employee information with the utmost confidentiality whenever possible.
  • Seek ways to implement and execute decisions that would honour employees’ interests.
  • Respect, honour, and accept an employee’s reaction to a decision that has been made without their consent; approach the situation with care and empathy for everyone involved, including the employee, yourself, the team, and bystanders.
  • Follow up on any agreements that have been made. If circumstances or decisions change, discuss the changes and continue to be transparent about the situation.

GC Workplace Accessibility Passport

Employees need to be aware that they are able to manage their image and reputation and have the option to not share certain information with their managers, especially of a personal nature. The GC Workplace Accessibility Passport offers an example of a solution in the public service that ensures management has enough information to support an employee and that the employee does not share unnecessary information.

The GC Workplace Accessibility Passport is a tool that facilitates conversations about an employee’s workplace adjustment needs. Through the passport, the employee describes how they interact with their work environment and the barriers they face, without having to self-identify or describe their medical diagnosis or health condition. The employee can then share the passport with their manager to discuss the solutions that will help the employee succeed at work and be at their best.

The GC Workplace Accessibility Passport travels with employees and is updated when an employee’s circumstances change. The passport facilitates employee mobility between organizations and avoids repeated requests for documentation. To learn more about this tool, check out the videos below.
