Mental health statistics in the federal public service

Here are some highlights about mental health in the federal public service from the 2020 Public Service Employee Survey (PSES), the most recent PSES results available. According to the survey results, public service employees who are part of an equity-seeking group are the most negatively impacted employees in terms of mental health in the workplace.[10]

Description of graphic

  • The biggest mental health gap (24%) is between public service employees with intellectual disabilities and public service employees overall in describing their workplace as psychologically healthy, with 68% of employees overall agreeing with this statement, compared to 44% of employees with intellectual disabilities.
  • When it comes to initiating a formal recourse process (grievance, complaint or appeal), 32% of those with sensory or environmental disabilities, 48% of those of mixed origin,  and 39% of those who are gender diverse feel they could initiate a formal recourse without fear of reprisal, compared to 55% of public service employees overall.
  • In terms of trust in senior management, 59% of Black employees, 72% of Chinese employees, and 79% of public service employees overall would feel comfortable sharing concerns about issues related to racism in the workplace with a person of authority, that is, their immediate supervisor, senior manager, ombud or human resources advisor.
  • A total of 61% of employees with intellectual disabilities report that their immediate supervisor supports their mental health and well-being, compared to 79% of public service employees overall; similarly, 75% of employees of mixed origin, 75% of Korean employees and 70% of gender diverse employees report that their immediate supervisor supports their mental health and well-being, compared to 79% of public service employees overall.

This infographic shows that while we’re making many good choices to create a workplace that is aware of the importance of mental health, there is still work to be done. Talking about mental health, discussing strategies to relieve stress and showing sensitivity to others’ mental health all make a difference.

The following Government of Canada drivers can play an important role in supporting the mental health  of public service employees who are members of equity-seeking groups:

  • Call to Action on Anti-Racism, Equity, and Inclusion
  • Federal Public Service Workplace Mental Health Strategy
  • Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada
  • Knowledge Circle for Indigenous Inclusion
  • Canada Labour Code
