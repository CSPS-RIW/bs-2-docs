Supporting a colleague at work

If you are concerned about a colleague at work who might be feeling mentally unwell, it’s important to keep the following principles in mind when offering support.

Start with a conversation

First, initiate a conversation to allow your colleague to tell you how they are feeling. Some people may be relieved to talk about what they are going through. Others may appreciate your efforts, even if they do not feel comfortable sharing.

Start the conversation honestly and slowly, and let your colleague lead it as much as possible. Listening can be a powerful tool and support. Stay away from labelling people and their experiences.

Whether your colleague would like to share or not is their decision. Do not force anyone to talk. If you have a duty to report or there are other constraints affecting your ability to maintain confidentiality, let your colleague know about these limitations before engaging in conversation.

Treat everyone with respect and understanding

Treating colleagues with respect is especially important when offering support. Be wary of subtle acts that can come across as disrespectful and be careful not to make assumptions or use words that may make them feel bad. Remember that everyone’s context is different. Practise understanding and compassion without judgment.

Encourage people to seek the support they need

You cannot force a colleague to seek formal or informal mental health support, but you can encourage them to do so—even if you never learn the outcome of your efforts to reach out.

Ask directly how you can help

It’s better to ask your colleague directly how you can help than to act based on your assumptions. How you would want to be treated in a similar situation might not be appropriate for everyone.

Respect your boundaries and take care of yourself

Supporting a person with mental health issues can be challenging and draining. It can also trigger your own negative emotions. Be aware of your limits, draw the necessary boundaries, and engage in self-care.
