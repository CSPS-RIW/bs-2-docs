Mental health and intersectionality

It is important to reflect on how members of diverse groups can have a different experience of mental health based on their identity and experience of oppression. This includes discrimination against Indigenous Peoples, racism, discrimination based on religion and culture such as Islamophobia and antisemitism, ableism, ageism, and discrimination against members of the 2SLGBTQI+ community. Intersectionality, a term coined in 1989 by scholar and civil rights activist Kimberlé Crenshaw, helps us to think about how overlapping social factors that comprise a person’s identity, such as race and gender, intersect to create unique disadvantages and discrimination.[4]

When it comes to mental health, intersectionality enables us to consider how multiple inequities compound, resulting in asymmetrical and inequitable mental health risks and protective factors, supports and barriers, and outcomes.

Adopting an intersectional perspective on mental health equips us to consider the range and diversity of mental health experiences, and to offer information, tools, and resources that are as inclusive as possible. In this course, mental health experiences, barriers, and supports will be contextualized through descriptions, data, scenarios, and reflections.
